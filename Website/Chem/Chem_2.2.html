Anisotropic Properties

• It can be defined as a difference in material’s physical or mechanical properties (like refractive index, conductivity, tensile strength) in different directions.
• Crystalline solids are anisotropic in nature. This anisotropy in crystals is due to different arrangement of particles along different directions.


A → B: Regular pattern
C → D: Regular pattern
But pattern in A—B is different from C—D

Isotropic Properties

• Amorphous Solids are isotropic on nature. It is because there is no long-range order in them and arrangement is regular along all the directions.
• So, value of any physical property would be same along any direction.

Classification of Crystalline Solids

1. Ionic solids
2. Covalent/network solids
3. Metallic solids
4. Molecular solids


1. Ionic solids
• Ions are the constituent particles of ionic solids. Such solids are formed by the 3-dimensional arrangements of cations and anions bound by electrostatic force. These solids are hard and brittle in nature.
• They have high melting and boiling points.
• In these solids, ions are not free to move. So, they are electrical insulators in solid state.
• But in molten state or when dissolved in water the ions become free to move about and they conduct electricity.

2. Covalent/network solids
• Crystalline solids of non-metals result in the formation of covalent bonds between adjacent atoms.
• For example: Diamond, Silicone carbonate, Graphite, etc.

3. Metallic solids
• Metals are collection of positive ions surrounded by and held together by a sea of free atoms electrons.
• For example: Na metal crystals

4. Molecular solids

     1) Non-Polar Molecular Solids
     • This solid is formed by either atoms (Ar, He) or molecules formed by non-Polar covalent bonds (H2, Cl2)
     • In these solids other atoms or molecules are held by weak dispersion forces or London forces.
     • These solids are soft and non-conductor of electricity.
     • They have low melting points and are usually in liquid or gaseous state at room temperature and pressure.

     2) Polar Molecular Solids
     • The molecules of substances like CO2, HCL are formed by polar covalent bond. Molecules are held together by dipole-dipole interaction.
     • These solids are soft and non-conductor of electricity.
     • Melting points :- PMS > NPMS
     • At room temperature most of them are gases or liquid.

     3) H-bonded Molecular Solids
     • The molecules of such solids contain polar covalent bond between H & F, O or N atoms.
     • Strong H-bonding is present in H2O(solid): Ice
     • Non-conductor of electricity
     • At room temperature they are volatile Liquids or soft solids.
